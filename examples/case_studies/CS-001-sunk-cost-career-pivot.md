# CS-001: Sunk Cost Fallacy in a Career Pivot

## Context

A user had spent 8 years in a finance role. They were considering a career pivot to tech but kept returning to the same conclusion: "I've invested too much to leave now."

They had an MBA focused on finance, a CFA charter, and a professional network built entirely around banking. The pivot would mean starting at a junior level in a new field.

## Challenge

The stated problem ("Should I switch careers?") was masking the actual problem: **attachment to sunk costs was overriding forward-looking expected value.**

The user's framing treated their 8 years of finance experience as a *cost to protect* rather than a *foundation to leverage*. This is a textbook sunk cost fallacy — but knowing the name of the bias doesn't automatically fix it.

## Approach

### Protocols Used

- **Protocol 504 (Problem Framing)** — Separated the stated problem from the actual problem. The real question wasn't "should I switch?" but "am I staying for the right reasons?"
- **Protocol 330 (Economic Expected Value)** — Ran a forward-looking EEV calculation comparing both paths across 5, 10, and 20 year horizons, factoring in personal utility (fulfillment, stress, growth trajectory)
- **Protocol 75 (Synthetic Parallel Reasoning)** — Generated 4 independent perspectives (Domain Expert, Adversarial Skeptic, Cross-Domain Pattern Matcher, First Principles)

### Key Steps

1. **Reframe**: "What would you advise a friend in this exact situation?" — This bypasses the sunk cost bias by removing personal attachment.
2. **EEV Calculation**: Finance path had higher Math EV at year 5, but tech path had higher Economic EV at year 10+ due to growth ceiling in traditional banking.
3. **Reversibility Test**: The pivot was partially reversible — finance skills remain valid. This shifted it from a Life Decision System to an Execution System problem.

## Outcome

The user negotiated a lateral move into fintech (financial technology), which preserved their domain expertise while moving them toward the tech stack. This was a **third option** that neither "stay" nor "leave" had captured.

- No salary reduction
- Retained CFA network relevance
- Gained exposure to engineering workflows
- 18 months later, had transitioned fully to a product management role in tech

## Pattern

**Pattern Name**: The Sunk Cost Lateral Move

**When to Apply**: When someone frames a decision as binary (stay/leave) and their primary resistance is loss of invested time/credentials.

**When NOT to Apply**: When the current situation involves active harm (burnout, toxicity) — in those cases, the Survival System should take priority over optimization.

## Lessons Learned

- The first answer to "Should I switch?" is almost never yes or no — it's "What's the actual problem?"
- Sunk costs are real in psychology even when they're irrational in economics. Acknowledge the feeling, then calculate around it.
- Most binary decisions have a third option that hasn't been explored yet.
