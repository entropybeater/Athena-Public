# CS-002: Scope Creep Destroys a Freelance Project's Margins

## Context

A freelance developer quoted $2,500 for a 3-page marketing website. The client agreed. Over the next 4 weeks, the project ballooned to 8 pages, a custom CMS, a contact form with CRM integration, and "a few animations."

The developer was 60 hours in — far past the 20-hour estimate — and hadn't invoiced for the extra work.

## Challenge

Two problems compounded:

1. **No written scope boundary**: The original agreement was verbal. "3-page website" was never defined with exclusions.
2. **People-pleasing override**: The developer kept saying "sure, I can add that" to maintain the relationship, effectively trading margin for comfort.

This is a classic **Form-Substance Gap** (Protocol 112): the *form* of the project ("a website") stayed the same, but the *substance* (scope, complexity, hours) tripled.

## Approach

### Protocols Used

- **Protocol 504 (Problem Framing)** — Identified that the real problem wasn't "the client keeps asking for more" but "I have no enforcement mechanism for scope boundaries."
- **Protocol 43 (Micro-Commit)** — Applied to project delivery: break the work into atomic, billable increments instead of one lump sum.
- **Protocol 112 (Form-Substance Gap)** — Named the gap explicitly: "This project has the form of a $2,500 website but the substance of a $7,000 web application."

### Key Steps

1. **Stop work immediately**. Send a professional email itemizing the current scope vs. original scope.
2. **Produce a Change Order**: List every addition with its estimated hours and cost. Total: $4,200 in additional work.
3. **Offer two paths**: (A) Complete the expanded scope for $6,700 total, or (B) deliver the original 3-page scope for $2,500 and scope the rest as Phase 2.
4. **For future projects**: Move to milestone-based pricing with a written scope document and explicit exclusion list.

## Outcome

The client pushed back initially but agreed to a reduced change order of $3,800 (original + $1,300 for the most critical additions). Not ideal — the developer absorbed ~$1,400 in uncompensated work.

However, the **process improvement** was the real win:

- All subsequent projects used a scope document with explicit exclusions
- Milestone billing (25% upfront, 25% at each checkpoint, 25% on delivery)
- A "Change Order" template was created and reused 12 times in the following year

## Pattern

**Pattern Name**: The Scope Ratchet

**When to Apply**: When a project has grown beyond its original boundaries but the price hasn't changed. Detectable by asking: "Would I quote this price if the client asked for this scope from day one?"

**When NOT to Apply**: When the additions are genuinely trivial (<30 min each) and the relationship has long-term strategic value.

## Lessons Learned

- Scope creep isn't a client problem — it's a boundary enforcement problem. The client will always ask for more. The question is whether you have a system for saying "yes, and that costs X."
- Verbal agreements are not agreements. They are vibes.
- The Form-Substance Gap is the diagnostic tool: if the label stays the same but the work triples, the gap is the problem.
